Why "switch to a proctored exam" isn't treated as a solution here

Locking down the exam room changes the opportunity to use AI, not necessarily the desire to — it's an escalating, imperfect arms race (students have been caught using hidden earpieces and smartwatches even under supervision), and it carries real surveillance and equity costs of its own. Several sources make this case directly: "In-Person Classes Aren't Safe From AI Cheating Boom" (Inside Higher Ed), "The AI-resistant classroom is a myth" (eSchool News), the Canadian Journal of Learning and Technology's piece on the academic-integrity "arms race", and a broader ethics literature on algorithmic proctoring.

The gold standard for this catalog is evidence that students changed their own AI use by choice — because a redesigned assessment made reaching for it pointless or unappealing — not evidence that they were simply prevented from trying. Strategies that only remove the opportunity, without addressing why students wanted to use AI in the first place, aren't catalogued here as a fix.

Notes on this survey

  • The bar for inclusion here is stricter than the main catalog: the source must report an actual measured drop in AI use or academic-integrity incidents (e.g. a before/after comparison, a breach-count, a misconduct-case tally) — not just researcher opinion that a strategy "should" or "probably did" reduce AI use.
  • Reducing AI use turns out to be a genuinely rare thing to find good evidence for. Most "strategy to cut AI use" content online is a proposal or framework with no outcome data — those are excluded, even when the idea itself is well-argued.
  • All three entries here currently rely on academic-integrity/misconduct case counts as their measure of "AI use going down," rather than direct usage data (e.g. student self-report, detection-tool flag rates). That's a real limitation worth keeping in mind when weighing how strong this evidence is.
  • Where a source is paywalled beyond its abstract, that's noted directly in the entry's evidence text, since it affects how much the headline claim can be verified.
