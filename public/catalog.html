Notes on the field survey

  • Two Padlet boards were checked for named case studies (Houston H. Harte Center, W&L — link; University of Dundee CTIL — link) and excluded: both are curated link libraries of tools, guidance and research, not first-person accounts of specific practice.
  • Curtin University's Educator Case Studies and UCL's Generative AI case studies are real repositories with genuine entries, but both render their lists client-side behind filters that aren't machine-readable from outside — worth browsing directly rather than treating as excluded.
  • Two Jisc series are catalogued separately: the AI in Marking & Feedback Pilot (deep-dive reports) and Innovative Insights (short community submissions) — same publisher, different projects.
  • Advance HE publishes its assessment & feedback compendium annually; the 2025 and 2026 editions were supplied directly as PDFs and are catalogued as one collection here, distinguished only by each entry's publication year. Of the 15 case studies across both editions, only 7 described a specific activity that was actually run with enough detail to populate every attribute — the rest were institutional frameworks (one explicitly "not yet formally piloted") or, in one case, an authentic-assessment redesign with no generative AI involved at all. All entries link to the compendium's knowledge-hub landing page.
  • A submitted "101 Creative Ideas" PowerPoint first turned out to be a blank contributor template; the actual 168-page #creativeHE collection was supplied afterwards. Of its ~100 crowdsourced submissions, most described a proposed idea ("could be used to…") rather than something the contributor had actually built, run or trialled — only 38 met the same bar applied everywhere else in this catalog and are included.
  • Every entry was re-screened against one test: is this someone using generative AI to do something, or guidance/policy/training about AI use? 21 case studies across ANTF, Durham, QAA and Utrecht that turned out to be institutional frameworks, literacy-building programmes, or policy documents (e.g. an AI assessment-scale adoption, or capacity-building workshops) were removed on this basis; 4 more QAA entries were retagged once a closer read of the source PDF surfaced the concrete tool actually being used underneath a policy-sounding title.
  • "Year published" reflects when the source document or post went up, not necessarily when the activity itself ran (which is sometimes a year or two earlier).
  • The five University of Sydney articles are almost all roundups covering several named academics each; every distinct example is logged as its own specimen rather than one entry per article.
  • Functionality (facets, free-text search) modeled on Stanford Accelerator for Learning's AI repository.
